While you're reading this, something historic is happening in northwestern Colombia.

Less than 350 kilometers from Medellín, three massive infrastructure projects are converging at the same time: a world-class port that has already received its first cargo vessel, the longest vehicular tunnel in Latin America about to open, and a fourth-generation highway network cutting an eight-hour journey down to four.

The destination at the end of all this is Urabá. And at the far end of the new route, beyond the ports and the banana trucks, there's a small Caribbean village where you can walk to Panama, swim in crystal-clear water, and fall asleep to the sound of the sea.

It's called Sapzurro. And it's about to stop being a secret.

---

## The Road Revolution: From 8 Hours to 4

To understand what's coming, you have to understand what was. Until recently, getting from Medellín to the Urabá coast meant an eight-plus-hour journey over a winding mountain road crossing the Cordillera Occidental — a route so demanding that most travelers simply ruled it out.

That's changing. Fast.

![A coastal highway cut through mountain rock — the kind of infrastructure transforming Urabá's connection to the world](/images/blog/uraba-boom/tunnel-toyo.jpg)

### Highway to the Sea 1: Already Operational

The first leg of the corridor — 181 kilometers between Medellín and Santa Fe de Antioquia — is now fully operational. It includes the Túnel de Occidente and dual carriageways that have already cut significant time off the first stretch of the coastal route.

### Highway to the Sea 2: 99.5% Complete

The 254 kilometers between Cañasgordas and Necoclí — the most complex section of the entire route — are 99.5% complete. We're talking about 62 bridges, 12 tunnels, and hundreds of kilometers of new road carved through mountains that were previously impassable. This stretch alone saves over two hours of travel time.

### Túnel del Toyo: The Longest Tunnel in Latin America

Then there's the centerpiece of the puzzle.

The **Túnel Guillermo Gaviria Echeverri** — known as the Túnel del Toyo — will be, at **9.73 kilometers**, the longest vehicular tunnel in all of Latin America. The full road complex includes two main tunnels, 32 viaducts, 20 secondary tunnels, and three interchanges — over 37 kilometers of infrastructure in total.

The main tunnel is already excavated and lined. Section 1 is over 99% complete. The Antioquia government took over Section 2 and has pushed it from 53% to 70% completion in a single year.

Estimated opening? **Late 2026 to early 2027.**

The conclusion is clear: the Medellín–Urabá journey will drop from eight-plus hours to **four to four-and-a-half hours**. A shift the region hasn't seen since the original Túnel de Occidente opened in 2006.

## Puerto Antioquia: Urabá Is Now a World-Class Port

If the Túnel del Toyo is the artery, Puerto Antioquia is the heart.

![Puerto Antioquia: New Panamax cargo vessels now operating in the Gulf of Urabá](/images/blog/uraba-boom/puerto-antioquia.jpg)

On **February 5, 2026**, Puerto Antioquia officially began commercial operations in the municipality of Turbo, following authorization from the Ministry of Transport. The first vessels are already loading banana containers bound for Europe.

The numbers are staggering:

- Investment of over **$800 million USD**
- A **1,340-meter** dock with five berth positions
- Capacity to receive **New Panamax** vessels (the largest ships transiting the Panama Canal)
- Target of moving **7 million tons** of cargo per year

But what matters for tourism isn't the containers. It's what Puerto Antioquia represents: definitive validation that Urabá has stopped being Antioquia's forgotten periphery and become a **first-tier economic hub**.

Projections point to a 7–9% increase in regional GDP, over 800 new businesses, and 17,000 indirect jobs created. And five more ports are in development in the area.

When infrastructure arrives, tourism follows. Always.

## The Tourism Effect: What's Happening Right Now

Urabá already receives over **600,000 visitors per year** by land and river. The Carepa airport moved over 186,000 passengers in 2024, and the number keeps climbing.

The region now has over 600 accommodation establishments and more than 7,000 beds. Hotel chains like Accor have already opened in Apartadó. In Necoclí, new boutique hotels are transforming the offer.

The Urabá–Darién Caribbean Tourism Corporation already has a master tourism agenda projected to 2040.

The official goal: **double the number of annual visitors to Urabá.**

## And at the End of the Road: Sapzurro

This is where the story gets interesting.

When you search Urabá on Google, you find ports, highways, and bananas. But travelers who already know the region understand something the headlines don't tell you: at the far end of the Gulf of Urabá, reached by speedboat from Necoclí or by small plane to Acandí, there's a corner of the Colombian Caribbean that feels like another planet.

![Aerial view of Sapzurro Bay — Colombia's last village before Panama](/images/sapzurro/bay-panorama.jpg)

**Sapzurro is Colombia's last village before Panama.** A bay surrounded by jungle-covered mountains, turquoise water, no cars, no noise, no traffic lights. A place where you can hike 20 minutes through the rainforest and arrive at La Miel — a Panamanian beach that consistently ranks among the most beautiful on the continent.

Capurganá, its more well-known neighbor, receives most of the tourists. But Sapzurro has something Capurganá can't offer: absolute silence, an intimate scale, and the genuine feeling of having discovered something the rest of the world doesn't know about yet.

That's going to change.

### Why Sapzurro Will Be the Next Great Destination

Think of it this way:

**Today**, getting to Sapzurro from Medellín takes around 12–14 hours (8+ hours by bus to Necoclí or Turbo, plus the boat). Only the most adventurous travelers attempt it.

**In 2027**, the same journey will take 5–6 hours total. Leave Medellín in the morning, pass through the Túnel del Toyo in minutes, take the Highway to the Sea 2 to Necoclí, and by 2pm you're sitting on the beach with a coconut in hand.

That changes everything.

Suddenly, Sapzurro goes from "that remote place someone mentioned" to a **viable long weekend from Medellín**. Travel agencies that currently sell Capurganá as the main destination with Sapzurro as a half-day excursion are going to rethink their model.
